# Product Targets: Binchotan / Hanmaru / Maple Lump

This note records product/source assumptions for comparing purchased charcoal
samples against the research datasets.

## Target Samples

| Target | Working interpretation | Best supporting sources |
|---|---|---|
| Kishu Hanmaru binchotan | Treat as Kishu binchotan in a split/half-round physical grade. Hanmaru is a shape/size descriptor, not a separate chemical charcoal class. | Wakayama Prefecture Kishu Binchotan grade table; Bincho.jp Hanmaru product page; Kachofugetsu and Konro product pages. |
| Generic Japanese binchotan | Treat as high-temperature white charcoal, often associated with Ubame oak / ubamegashi when sold as Kishu binchotan. | Wakayama Prefecture Kishu Binchotan page; Pereira UFSCar thesis; Chia et al. white-charcoal characterization. |
| Costco / Nature's Own maple lump | Treat as ordinary black hardwood lump charcoal, maple/sugar-maple feedstock. | Costco Canada Nature's Own listing; Costco Business Centre listing; Basques manufacturer FAQ. |

## Product-Source Notes

- Wakayama Prefecture describes Kishu Binchotan as a regional collective
  trademark and lists grades by quality, wood species, and original log
  diameter. The table includes Hanmaru entries as split pieces.
- Bincho.jp describes Hanmaru as thick raw wood split into half or quarter
  pieces before carbonization, giving a half-moon or triangular cross-section.
- Product pages for Kishu Hanmaru commonly state Ubame oak / ubamegashi, but
  Hanmaru itself should be interpreted as a physical grade.
- Costco Canada's Nature's Own listing states the lump charcoal is made from
  sugar maple logs and is a Canadian natural hardwood lump charcoal.
- Basques' FAQ describes its hardwood charcoal as Quebec hardwood, mainly sugar
  maple, made by slow pyrolysis of logs.

## Analysis Implication

The strongest expected contrast is maple lump versus Kishu/binchotan white
charcoal. Hanmaru versus other Kishu binchotan may show less FTIR chemistry
difference if both are genuine high-temperature Ubame-oak binchotan; shape and
piece size are more likely to affect burn behavior than bulk chemistry.
